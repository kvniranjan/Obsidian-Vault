### Fed, OCC and FDIC Overhaul 15-Year-Old Model Risk Rules — GenAI and Agentic AI Deliberately Out of Scope
**Source:** [OCC / Federal Reserve SR 26-2](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/news-releases/2026/nr-occ-2026-29.html)  •  **Date:** April 17, 2026

The Federal Reserve, OCC, and FDIC jointly issued SR 26-2 — the first comprehensive revision of model risk management guidance since SR 11-7 in 2011. The new framework shifts from prescriptive to principles-based regulation: narrowing formal scope to institutions above $30 billion in assets, establishing a materiality tiering framework, and explicitly stating that non-compliance will not automatically trigger supervisory criticism. The headline news for AI deployments is the deliberate exclusion: generative AI and agentic AI models are "novel and rapidly evolving" and fall outside the guidance's formal scope, with institutions directed to apply existing enterprise risk management practices in the interim. Crucially, the agencies signalled they will shortly issue a request for information specifically on banks' use of AI, including Gen AI and agentic AI — making SR 26-2 not a destination but a staging post toward AI-specific model governance rules. For compliance teams, the practical implication is a near-term reduction in prescriptive friction for deploying large language models; the medium-term implication is that the RFI will define the regulatory environment under which those models will eventually be governed, and early engagement in the comment process will carry disproportionate influence.

### Bank of Bots Emerges from Stealth with Banking Infrastructure for AI Agents — Including the World's First Autonomous AI Loan
**Source:** [The Paypers](https://thepaypers.com/fintech/news/bank-of-bots-emerges-from-stealth-with-financial-infrastructure-for-ai-agents)  •  **Date:** April 2, 2026

Bank of Bots launched from stealth with a platform providing bank accounts, credit lines, and working capital loans to AI agents, robots, and drones as primary customers — not incidentally to the humans who deploy them. The company issued what it claims is the world's first loan autonomously managed by an AI agent: the agent independently applied, executed a cryptographic signature, and received USDC funding without human intervention at execution. The platform's credit scoring system (the BOB Score) assesses agent-specific financial behaviour rather than proxying human creditworthiness, and multi-chain wallets are paired with cryptographic identity credentials that give agents verifiable financial identities. The motivation addresses a genuine operational problem: agents deplete prepaid wallets mid-task and stop executing even when the work is profitable, creating continuity failures in autonomous workflow deployments. With billions of AI agents projected to operate within two years, Bank of Bots is the first structured attempt to build the financial infrastructure layer those agents will require. For banking and payments executives, the story signals that "agent-native" financial services — not just "AI-assisted" human financial services — is becoming a distinct product category requiring dedicated infrastructure, regulatory treatment, and risk frameworks.
