### Citi Builds Arc as the Control Layer for Internal AI Agents
**Source:** [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/04/30/exclusive-citi-moves-into-agentic-ai)  •  **Date:** April 30, 2026

Citi is rolling out Arc, an internal platform that lets employees build and deploy AI agents through one secured operating environment. This is not another generic enterprise chatbot story. Axios reports that 180,000 Citi employees already use enterprise AI tools backed by leading models, but Arc is intended to centralize agent creation, use-case management, monitoring, and intervention. That matters because banks are moving from isolated AI assistance to coordinated agent workflows that can compile portfolio data, analyze market trends, and run scenario tests. The risk is obvious: once agents can take multi-step actions across internal systems, governance cannot sit in scattered team-level controls. Citi is trying to put the agent layer where managers can observe behavior and stop tasks when needed. For financial services leaders, the useful signal is architectural. The next phase of bank AI will be judged less by which model a firm licenses and more by whether it has a controlled agent runtime, permission boundaries, audit trails, and escalation paths. Without that, agentic AI is just shadow automation with a better brand name.

### UK Financial Firms Warn AI Compliance Standards Are Missing
**Source:** [FinTech Global](https://fintech.global/2026/04/30/senior-leaders-warn-of-critical-ai-compliance-shortfall/)  •  **Date:** April 30, 2026

FinTech Global covered new Zango AI research warning that UK and European financial institutions lack a shared operating standard for AI governance. The report is based on interviews with 27 senior leaders and roundtables with 60 additional practitioners, including representatives from Santander, Stripe, Standard Chartered, Lloyds, Monzo, Allica Bank, Commerzbank, Revolut, and Ecommpay. The core problem is not abstract regulation. Business and technology teams are deploying generative and agentic tools faster than risk and compliance functions can inventory or govern them. Several institutions reportedly could not account for all AI tools in active use. That is a brutal finding for a regulated sector that talks constantly about control. The report also points to $579 billion in global fraud losses in 2025 and says 90% of financial professionals report rising AI-enabled attacks. The blunt takeaway: firms waiting for perfect regulator-written AI guidance are already behind. They need internal AI inventories, use-case tiering, model ownership, monitoring, and incident playbooks now. "Principles-based" AI governance is useless if nobody can say which systems are live.

### FIDO Starts Standards Work for Agent Authentication and Agent Payments
**Source:** [FIDO Alliance](https://fidoalliance.org/fido-alliance-to-develop-standards-for-trusted-ai-agent-interactions/) / [Google](https://blog.google/products-and-platforms/platforms/google-pay/agent-payments-protocol-fido-alliance/)  •  **Date:** April 28-29, 2026

The FIDO Alliance has launched standards work for trusted AI agent interactions and agent-initiated commerce, with technical contributions from Google and Mastercard. Google is donating its Agent Payments Protocol to FIDO and released AP2 v0.2 with support for "Human Not Present" payments, where agents can execute purchases based on pre-authorized user instructions. Mastercard's Verifiable Intent framework is also being contributed as an accountability layer for user-authorized agent actions. This is the payments industry's agent problem getting real. Card networks, issuers, merchants, wallets, and AI platforms need a common way to prove that a human authorized an agent, that the agent acted within scope, and that the resulting transaction can be inspected later without exposing more data than necessary. FIDO is the right venue because it already has credibility in phishing-resistant authentication. But standards work will not save banks that have not mapped their own delegated-authority model. Issuers need to decide how agent permissions, spend limits, merchant restrictions, dispute evidence, and fraud scoring will actually work inside live payment flows.

### AI Productivity Gains Still Depend on Boring Operating Work
**Source:** [FinTech Global / Prometeia](https://fintech.global/2026/04/30/can-ai-deliver-real-productivity-gains/)  •  **Date:** April 30, 2026

FinTech Global summarized Prometeia analysis on whether generative AI can deliver measurable productivity gains. The early evidence is positive at the task level, especially in writing, coding, and text analysis, with less experienced workers often seeing the strongest improvement. But the wider productivity story is more complicated. Jobs are bundles of activities, not neat automation units, and firm-level gains depend on complementary investment in software, data management, training, and organizational redesign. That is directly relevant to banks, insurers, payment firms, and wealth managers because the industry has no shortage of AI pilots. The shortage is production discipline. If a financial institution drops generative AI into broken workflows, fragmented data, and unclear accountability, the output will be scattered efficiency theater. If it pairs AI with process redesign, clean data access, risk controls, and manager training, the gains can compound. This is the part executives like least because it is not flashy: productivity comes from redesigning work, not sprinkling AI over old operating models.
