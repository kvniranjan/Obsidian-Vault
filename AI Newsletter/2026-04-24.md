### QBE and Beazley Cap AI-Related Cyber Payouts at ~10% of Policy Value
**Source:** [Life Insurance International](https://www.lifeinsuranceinternational.com/news/insurers-cap-ai-related-payouts/)  •  **Date:** April 23, 2026

Two major specialty insurers are structurally restricting cyber coverage for AI-related losses. QBE introduced sub-limits for large language model (LLM) jacking — a growing attack class in which adversaries hijack AI models to exfiltrate data or commit fraud — capping those losses at roughly 5% of total policy value (a $250,000 sub-limit on a $5 million policy in their example). Beazley similarly designed new contract terms limiting liability for AI-related regulatory breaches, with its AI sub-limits set at approximately 10% of overall policy value; the firm noted the amendments were still being finalised. AIG separately is seeking regulatory approval to exclude AI losses from standard policies entirely. The practical effect for financial institutions: the coverage layer they assumed existed for AI-driven incidents is being silently reduced or removed, precisely as AI deployments — and the attack surface they create — expand. Brokers and legal advisors warned the QBE and Beazley provisions could be read broadly enough to restrict coverage for emerging AI risks beyond LLM jacking. This story is a direct sequel to insurers' Q4 2025 regulatory filings for exclusions; institutions relying on cyber policy language written before 2025 should audit their actual coverage against today's AI risk landscape.

### Consumer Support for AI in P&C Insurance Nearly Doubles — But Comfort Stops at Routine Tasks
**Source:** [Business Wire / Insurity](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260421860636/en/Consumer-Support-for-AI-in-PC-Insurance-Nearly-Doubles-in-2026-Insurity-Survey-Finds)  •  **Date:** April 21, 2026

Insurity's 2026 consumer AI survey — 1,000+ U.S. adults, conducted February 2026 — found that 39% of respondents now say it is a good idea for their insurer to use AI to improve services, nearly double the 20% who said so in 2025. Daily AI tool usage among consumers reached 27% (versus near-zero awareness two years ago), and resistance to insurers that publicly use AI fell from 44% in 2025 to 36%. The headline shift, however, masks a sharp boundary: consumers are comfortable with AI generating a quote (46%), tracking a claim (39%), or updating personal information (38%) — but only 22% would allow AI to file a claim on their behalf, and just 16% are comfortable with AI autonomously canceling or renewing a policy. For P&C carriers designing agentic AI workflows, the survey defines the current legitimacy boundary: task-level AI assistance is achieving consumer acceptance, but decision-authority AI — specifically actions with binding financial or contractual consequences — faces significant resistance. Insurers that move AI into autonomous policy management or claims settlement without explicit customer consent frameworks risk eroding the trust gains that have taken two years to accumulate.

### Goldman Sachs Global Institute: "World Models" Are the Next Decisive AI Leap — With Capital Markets Stakes
**Source:** [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/04/23/goldman-sachs-ai-world-model-missing-link/)  •  **Date:** April 23, 2026

A new Goldman Sachs Global Institute paper authored by co-head George Lee and managing director Dan Keyserling argues that "world models" — AI systems that develop an internal, causal understanding of how physical and social environments actually behave, not just statistical patterns in text — represent the next qualitative leap beyond current large language models. The report distinguishes two architectures: physical world models (enabling robots and autonomous systems to practise failure safely in simulation) and virtual/social world models (digital environments populated by AI agents with goals and incentives, allowing enterprises to simulate how markets, competitors, and organisations respond to decisions). The capital markets implication is direct: institutions that build faithful reality simulations of their own market microstructure, counterparty behaviour, or liquidity dynamics gain a new class of strategic advantage. Lee and Keyserling also challenge prevailing AI infrastructure forecasts, arguing world model development will require substantially more compute than current projections assume — a signal relevant to bank technology budgeting and hyperscaler investment assessments for institutions with significant data centre or cloud commitments.
