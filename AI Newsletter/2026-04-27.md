### Agentic AI Moves From Demos Into Financial Services Desk Work
**Source:** [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2026/ai-agents-move-from-demo-day-to-desk-work/)  •  **Date:** April 27, 2026

PYMNTS pulled together current signals from Snowflake, KPMG, The Economist, and Oracle showing the same pattern across banks, insurers, and asset managers: agentic AI is moving into controlled desk work rather than open-ended autonomous judgment. The first useful applications are boring on purpose: gathering data, checking documents, monitoring signals, routing approvals, preparing recommendations, and supporting compliance reviews. That matters because the financial services AI discussion has been too obsessed with flashy agents that "do everything." The real adoption path is narrower and more practical. Snowflake frames the shift as moving from data used to understand problems toward data used to take action inside governed workflows. KPMG stresses reusable components, approval mechanisms, documentation, security, and audit controls. The Economist points to mortgage approvals, KYC, fraud detection, credit assessment, and audits as workflows where agents can compress manual steps while humans retain decision authority. For institutions, the takeaway is simple: agentic AI will not fix broken processes by magic. The firms that win will redesign the workflow, wire in controls, and measure whether the agent actually removes handoffs.

### Affirm Frames "Agentic Credit" as the Next Consumer Lending Model
**Source:** [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/consumer-finance/2026/affirm-agentic-credit-rewrites-the-rules-of-consumer-lending/)  •  **Date:** April 27, 2026

Affirm president Libor Michalek argued that "agentic credit" lets lenders underwrite individual purchases in real time instead of relying on static revolving credit lines built around older data constraints. The idea is transaction-level underwriting: evaluate this consumer, this purchase, this price, and this cash-flow context at the moment of decision. Affirm's argument is that AI-driven decisioning can both expand access for consumers who are invisible to traditional credit models and decline purchases that would overextend the buyer. That is a sharper product thesis than generic "AI credit scoring." It reframes underwriting from a periodic identity-based assessment into a real-time affordability and risk calculation embedded at checkout. Banks and card issuers already offer installment features, but Affirm is attacking the clunky post-purchase version of that model. For consumer lenders, the threat is obvious: if transparent, transaction-level underwriting keeps scaling, revolving credit loses more ground to embedded financing that tells consumers the full cost before they buy. The uncomfortable part for banks is that the AI layer does not just improve underwriting. It changes where the credit relationship begins.

### Agentic Commerce Forces a Rethink of Card Infrastructure
**Source:** [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/news/payments-innovation/2026/agentic-commerce-forces-a-rethink-of-card-infrastructure/)  •  **Date:** April 27, 2026

PYMNTS Intelligence and Paymentology argue that AI agents initiating transactions on behalf of consumers will strain card infrastructure built for human-initiated, linear purchases. Nearly half of consumers express interest in AI agents handling tasks such as grocery shopping or meal planning, but the existing payments stack is not designed for high-velocity, cross-platform, machine-driven transaction flows. The weak points are authorization, granular spending controls, real-time context, identity verification, fraud detection, and compliance. Traditional fraud systems model human behavior; they are less useful when the actor is a machine operating at machine speed. The proposed answer is intelligent card infrastructure: programmable controls, tokenized credentials, real-time decisioning, and cloud/API architectures that let issuers manage transaction context dynamically. This is not a distant payments theory problem. Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, Ramp, and others are already building around agentic commerce. Issuers that do not modernize will become bottlenecks, and bottlenecks in payments do not get sympathy. They get routed around.

Gupta's point is especially relevant for fraud prevention, where blunt rules historically forced firms to choose between lower fraud and higher customer friction. Green Dot also links this to financial inclusion: alternative and behavioral data can help build risk profiles for thin-file users who are excluded by rigid underwriting. The blunt read: financial firms sitting on disconnected data lakes do not have a moat. They have expensive storage. The moat is whether the firm can turn those signals into fast, governed decisions before competitors do.

For financial services technology teams, the relevant part is not the branding. The infrastructure layer is maturing fast. Bank control frameworks are not moving at the same speed.
