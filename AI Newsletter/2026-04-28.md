### Cambridge: 80%+ of Financial Firms Use AI, While Regulators Lag Badly
**Source:** [Cambridge Judge Business School](https://www.jbs.cam.ac.uk/2026/report-finds-uneven-ai-adoption-in-financial-services/) / [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2026/finance-industry-surpasses-regulators-in-ai-adoption/)  •  **Date:** April 28, 2026

The Cambridge Centre for Alternative Finance released its 2026 Global AI in Financial Services Report, and the blunt finding is that the industry has moved faster than the people supervising it. More than 80% of financial services firms are adopting AI at some level, 52% are experimenting with agentic AI, and firms are already seeing efficiency and profitability gains. Regulators are behind: PYMNTS reports that 48% of surveyed regulators are still only exploring AI or not engaged with it at all. The risk is not academic. Cambridge flags software engineering as the most mature industry AI use case and a primary cyber risk transmission vector, with adversarial AI, data privacy, model opacity, hallucinations, unreliable outputs, and market abuse all ranking as serious concerns. The vendor gap is also ugly: AI vendors appear less worried than banks and regulators about adversarial AI and cyber resilience. For bank boards, the lesson is simple. AI adoption is no longer the differentiator by itself. The differentiator is whether the institution can prove it understands the risks faster than supervisors eventually force the issue.

### Q2 Adds AI-Enabled Real-Time Account Takeover Controls
**Source:** [Q2 Holdings](https://investors.q2.com/news/investor-news/news-details/2026/Q2-Enhances-Account-Takeover-Protection-With-AI-Enabled-Detection-and-Real-Time-Response-Capabilities/default.aspx) / [FinTech Global](https://fintech.global/2026/04/28/q2-adds-real-time-fraud-detection-capabilities/)  •  **Date:** April 27-28, 2026

Q2 introduced User Activity Monitoring and Restricted Entitlements Mode, two capabilities designed to detect and contain account takeover attacks inside live digital banking sessions. User Activity Monitoring uses AI-assisted behavioral detection to identify high-risk patterns across session behavior, account activity, and transactions. Restricted Entitlements Mode is the enforcement layer: it can limit access, adjust permissions, or contain compromised accounts in real time. This matters because account takeover no longer happens at one checkpoint. It spreads across login, device behavior, profile changes, payments setup, and transaction execution. Banks that still treat fraud as a point-in-time transaction-monitoring problem are fighting yesterday's attack pattern. The more important signal here is architecture. Q2 is connecting detection and enforcement inside the same workflow, which cuts the lag between "we found something" and "we stopped something." First Bank said more than a third of User Activity Monitoring alerts in testing aligned to confirmed fraud. That is not proof of perfection, but it is enough to make the old manual queue model look painfully slow.

### AI Is Forcing Banks to Reopen Core Cloud Contracts
**Source:** [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/news/banking/2026/everything-is-ai-now-and-its-reshaping-banking-core-cloud-contracts/)  •  **Date:** April 28, 2026

PYMNTS argues that AI is changing the economics and control requirements behind banking cloud contracts. Legacy agreements were built around predictable storage, uptime, resilience, and scheduled compute. AI workloads are different: inference, data movement, privacy-critical pipelines, model operations, and real-time decisioning can demand more compute, more integration, and more flexibility than traditional banking modernization plans assumed. The practical issue for banks is vendor control. If core banking, data, model tooling, and observability are locked inside one provider's ecosystem, the bank may discover that its AI roadmap is constrained by contract terms negotiated for an earlier generation of cloud migration. Data sovereignty is also moving from legal appendix to board-level infrastructure issue, especially as regulators and central banks become more sensitive to where sensitive financial data is processed. The hard read: AI strategy cannot sit only with innovation teams. Procurement, legal, architecture, compliance, and third-party risk need to renegotiate around portability, jurisdictional controls, auditability, and exit options before AI becomes too embedded to move.

### Payments Firms Say AI-Fueled Fraud Now Requires AI-Led Defense
**Source:** [Payments Dive](https://www.paymentsdive.com/news/harnessing-ai-to-fight-fraud/818676/)  •  **Date:** April 28, 2026

Payments Dive coverage from Nacha's Smarter Faster Payments conference captures the current fraud mood: banks and payments companies increasingly believe AI is necessary to fight fraud that is itself being scaled by AI. The issue is not that fraud teams need another dashboard. It is that attack speed, personalization, and volume have changed. Voice cloning, synthetic identity, account takeover, phishing, mule recruitment, and social engineering are no longer constrained by manual criminal labor. That forces a defensive shift from static rules and post-event review to adaptive models, behavioral analytics, real-time signal sharing, and faster intervention. The interesting policy angle is that congressional activity may eventually give firms more room to use AI in fraud prevention. That sounds helpful, but banks should not wait for lawmakers to make basic risk decisions for them. Fraud teams need defensible AI governance now: clear model ownership, audit trails, bias checks, escalation paths, and evidence that automated decisions reduce losses without locking out legitimate customers.

### Banks Now Need to Know When a Bot Has Permission
**Source:** [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/data/2026/banks-need-to-know-when-the-bot-has-permission/)  •  **Date:** April 28, 2026

Entersekt co-founder and chief strategy officer Dewald Nolte told PYMNTS that the next authentication problem is intent, not just identity. Historically, bot behavior was treated as suspicious by default. That assumption breaks when consumers begin authorizing AI agents to shop, pay bills, compare products, or manage account tasks. Banks and payment firms will need to distinguish hostile automation from delegated automation. That requires better data context: what the customer authorized, under what conditions, against which spending limits, for which merchants, and through which device or agent identity. The real operational problem is fragmentation. If online banking, mobile banking, call center, card, and payment data sit in separate views, a fraudster can attack across channels while each system sees only part of the picture. AI can help unify signals, but only if the data is clean and contextual. The takeaway for financial institutions is blunt: "Know your customer" is no longer enough. The control layer must also know the customer's agent and know the customer's mandate.

### Galileo Says Modern Fraud Has Outgrown Static Rules
**Source:** [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/news/payments-innovation/2026/galileo-says-modern-fraud-has-outgrown-static-rules/)  •  **Date:** April 28, 2026

Galileo senior director of global payments risk management Max Spivakovsky told PYMNTS that payments leaders are pulling ahead by treating data as a decisioning system rather than a storage problem. That distinction is not semantic. Static rules struggle when fraud tactics shift quickly, customer behavior spans multiple channels, and AI-generated attacks adapt faster than a rules committee can meet. The better model is a feedback loop: unified data, real-time decisions, measured outcomes, and continuous adjustment. This is especially important for issuers and processors balancing fraud loss against false positives. Blocking more transactions can make a fraud metric look better while quietly destroying customer trust and approval rates. The broader signal is that payments risk is becoming an execution contest. Many firms can buy similar AI tooling. Fewer can connect the data, governance, enforcement, and learning loops needed to make the tooling consistently useful.

### CFTC Turns to AI for Crypto Registration Reviews and Market Surveillance
**Source:** [Crypto Briefing](https://cryptobriefing.com/cftc-ai-crypto-registration-tools) / [CoinMarketCap](https://coinmarketcap.com/academy/article/cftc-deploys-ai-to-speed-crypto-registration-reviews)  •  **Date:** April 28, 2026

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission is building AI tools to review crypto registration applications, flag incomplete submissions, and support market surveillance, according to reporting on comments from Chairman Mike Selig. The agency is also training staff on Microsoft Copilot and developing internal tools to review swap data and reach conclusions about trading activity. The context matters: the CFTC is trying to expand oversight of digital assets and prediction markets while operating after staff reductions of more than one-fifth. For financial services leaders, this is a useful supervisory technology signal. Regulators will use AI not only to understand industry AI, but to triage filings, monitor markets, and stretch limited staff capacity. That does not automatically mean better supervision. Bad automation inside a regulator can create its own problems. But firms should assume that future filings, surveillance data, and compliance narratives will increasingly be read by machines before humans. Sloppy submissions and weak audit trails will become easier to spot and harder to explain away.
