### Mastercard Says Payments Are Moving From KYC to KYA
**Source:** [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/mastercard/2026/mastercard-sees-data-moving-payments-from-kyc-to-kya/) - **Date:** May 1, 2026

Mastercard's Kaushik Gopal told PYMNTS that AI lets the network process multiple payment signals in milliseconds, combining behavioral patterns, geolocation, merchant context, transaction history, and post-event feedback into a composite decision. The story is useful because it connects two threads that banks often treat separately: fraud decisioning and agent identity. Mastercard's framing is that payment risk is shifting from simple customer identification toward "know your agent," where the transaction may be initiated by software acting under a human mandate. That introduces new data fields, new evidence needs, and new fraud models. The blunt point for issuers is that KYA cannot be a slogan. It requires a data model that captures who authorized the agent, what scope was granted, what transaction context changed, and how the institution will prove all of that later. Traditional KYC says who the customer is. Agentic commerce asks whether this specific autonomous action was inside the customer's intent. Those are not the same control problem.

### WEX Puts Fraud Detection on a 500-Millisecond Clock
**Source:** [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/fraud-prevention/2026/wex-puts-fraud-detection-on-a-500-millisecond-clock/) - **Date:** May 1, 2026

WEX's William Fitzgerald told PYMNTS that fraud teams no longer get hours or days to correlate signals. In payments, they may get 500 milliseconds to authenticate, score risk, and approve or decline. That is the reality behind the industry's obsession with AI fraud tooling. Static rules and manual review queues cannot keep pace with automated attacks, but sloppy AI creates its own false-positive and customer-friction problems. Fitzgerald's useful point is that fraud performance now depends on governed, enriched, contextual data. Behavioral biometrics, passkeys, transaction sequencing, device behavior, merchant constraints, and virtual-card controls all matter because they let firms intervene precisely instead of spraying friction across every user. For bank and payment executives, the takeaway is direct: real-time fraud defense is an architecture problem before it is a model problem. If the fraud stack cannot ingest the right signals, score them fast, and enforce controls inside the payment flow, AI branding will not save it.

The hard questions are familiar: who performed KYC or KYB, who owns dispute evidence, what happens when the agent exceeds scope, and whether the authorization trail is good enough for regulators and customers.

### AI Still Cannot Replace the Rules Engine in Tax Reporting
**Source:** [FinTech Global / Label](https://fintech.global/2026/05/01/why-ai-alone-wont-fix-your-tax-reporting-challenges/) - **Date:** May 1, 2026

FinTech Global covered Label's argument that AI can help tax reporting teams process documents, reconcile inconsistent data, and flag exceptions, but it cannot substitute for the control framework behind FATCA, CRS, and cross-border tax obligations. This is a needed correction to the lazy "AI will automate compliance" pitch. Tax reporting is not a vibe-based workflow. Firms need complete documentation, consistent data, deterministic rule application, and accurate reporting to authorities. AI is useful where it reduces manual extraction, maps messy inputs, or pushes anomalies to reviewers. It becomes dangerous when firms let probabilistic outputs pretend to be factual determinations. For compliance leaders, the practical line is simple: use AI to accelerate the workflow, not to invent the regulatory answer. The firms that get this right will pair AI with structured rules, exception queues, audit trails, and human accountability. The ones that do not will eventually discover that hallucinated confidence is not a defense in a regulatory exam.
